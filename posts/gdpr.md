In 2016, Europeans gained several important privacy rights in this age of information and data that
has seen many abuses like the [Cambridge Analytica scandal]. The [General Data Protection Regulation (2016/679)]
started an era of transparency in the technology industry that requires businesses to safeguard
customer information and to disclose when, how, what, and why they are processing that information.
The [Brussels effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brussels_effect) and the leadership the European Union has demonstrated when it comes to privacy
(with some exceptions, like E2E) has given rise to similar legislation around the world, resulting
in increased protections for people across the globe.

## Origins

The [EU Charter of Fundamental Rights], the EU's Bill of Rights, gives all EU residents the right to
data protection explicitly in Article 8.

> Everyone has the right to the protection of personal data concerning him or her.

Initially, the EU gave its people some privacy rights through the [1995 Data Protection Directive (95/46/EC)].
However, as a directive which sets out goals that EU countries must achieve, it did not have a universal
implementation and lacked strong controls to protect data in the online age. The directive contained
some limited rights, like the right of access and the right to object, but nothing like what is
necessary when companies like Meta (formerly Facebook), Google, RELX, and Thomson Reuters exist.

As a regulation, the GDPR was enacted directly with universal implementation across the bloc. After
being [proposed by the European Commission] (EC) in 2012 to update and modernize the principles enshrined
in the Directive. It took four years of work on the proposal to be finalized before the Council of
the European Union, the upper house of the EU legislature composed of appointed ministers from member
states. It passed 27 to 1 with Austria being [the lone holdout], saying that the GDPR "in some respects
falls short of that provided by the ... Data Protection Directive ... and its implementation in
national data protection law" and that it was "not possible to 'offset' these deficits" because the
intended form was an EU regulation. The lower house composed of directly-elected MEPs under proportional
representation, the European Parliament, passed the regulation a few days later, sending it for
publication in the *Official Journal of the European Union*. It entered force on May 24, 2016 and
began applying on May 25, 2018.

## Rights

The GDPR gives all residents the [right to know] what a controller/processor has collected about them,
the [right to correct] that information, the [right to restrict processing], the [right to be forgotten]
(also known as the right to erasure), and the right to data portability.

Data controllers are entities that "determines the purposes and means of the processing of personal data".
Data processors are entities that process personal data on behalf of a controller.

Controllers are required to provide information "without undue delay ... within one month of receipt".
The GDPR requires that information be provided free of charge, however when requests are "manifestly
unfounded or excessive", controllers may charge a reasonable fee or refuse to act.

## Enforcement

Controllers that do not comply with a request may be referred to a supervisory authority by lodging
a complaint and seeking a judicial remedy. A supervisory authority is a public authority set up by
each Member State to enforce and advance the GDPR and its principles that acts with complete independence.
They are also known as "data protection authorities" (DPAs). In select cases, cases can be sent to
the [European Data Protection Board] (EDPB), created by the GDPR, for dispute resolution. Otherwise,
the case can be sent to European courts.

Every data subject has the right to lodge a complaint with a supervisory authority, usually their
national authority. Effective judicial remedies are guaranteed against a legally binding decision of
a supervisory authority. If a DPA does not handle a complaint or does not inform the subject about
the progress or outcome of a complaint, then an effective judicial remedy is available. Data subjects
also have the right to an effective judicial remedy when they believe their GDPR rights have been
infringed upon.

## Cross-Border Data Transfers

The GDPR regulates data transfers from the EU and EEA to other countries. Third countries must have
an "adequate level of protection" to ensure that the level of data protection in the EU is not undermined.
